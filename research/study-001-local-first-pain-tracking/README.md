# Study 001: Local First Pain Tracking

This folder contains the research materials for **Study 001: Local First Pain Tracking**, an exploratory usability and trust study focused on PainTracker.

The study examines how people managing chronic pain interact with a local first pain tracking app, with attention to usability, perceived safety, privacy understanding, cognitive load, data control, and barriers to routine use.

This is not a clinical trial, treatment study, diagnostic study, or medical outcome study. It does not evaluate whether PainTracker reduces pain or improves health outcomes.

## Study Purpose

Study 001 asks whether a local first pain tracking tool can help users document pain while preserving privacy, control, and trust.

The study focuses on:

- Core pain logging usability
- Participant understanding of local data storage
- Trust in data control and privacy
- Perceived safety during sensitive self documentation
- Cognitive burden during first use
- Confidence in reviewing, correcting, and exporting records
- Barriers to routine logging

## Research Questions

1. Can participants complete core pain logging tasks without assistance?
2. Do participants understand where their data is stored?
3. Does the local first model increase perceived trust and control?
4. Which parts of the app create confusion, hesitation, or privacy concern?
5. Do participants feel confident reviewing, correcting, and exporting records?
6. What barriers may prevent routine use?

## Protective Computing Focus

Study 001 evaluates PainTracker against key Protective Computing principles:

| Principle | Study Focus |
|---|---|
| Local authority | Do participants understand and trust that they control their data? |
| Exposure minimization | Does the app avoid unnecessary disclosure? |
| Consent clarity | Do participants understand what is stored, exported, and shared? |
| Reversible state | Can participants correct, review, or recover from mistakes? |
| Explicit degradation | Do participants understand what happens if something fails? |
| Cognitive protection | Does the app reduce or increase mental burden? |

## Scope Limits

This study does not claim to prove:

- Clinical effectiveness
- Pain reduction
- Improved medical outcomes
- Long term adherence
- Superiority over all cloud based health apps

It may provide early evidence about:

- First use usability
- Perceived safety
- Trust in local first architecture
- Privacy understanding
- Export confidence
- Design changes needed before larger testing

## Core Principle

The research process must reflect the same protective values being tested in the software.

A study about safe local first tools cannot collect excessive data, pressure participants, obscure consent, or turn vulnerable users into an extraction surface.
