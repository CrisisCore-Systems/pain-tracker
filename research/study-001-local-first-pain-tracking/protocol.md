# Study Protocol

## Study Title

Local First Pain Tracking: Usability, Trust, and Protective Experience Evaluation

## Study Type

Mixed methods usability and trust study.

This is not a clinical trial, medical treatment study, diagnostic study, or assessment of pain improvement. The study evaluates first use experience, privacy understanding, perceived safety, cognitive load, and barriers to routine adoption.

## Study Objective

The objective of this study is to evaluate how adults with chronic pain interact with a local first pain tracking app, with specific focus on:

1. Usability of core pain logging tasks.
2. Participant understanding of local first data storage.
3. Trust in data control and privacy.
4. Perceived safety while documenting sensitive health related information.
5. Cognitive burden during first use.
6. Confidence in reviewing, correcting, and exporting records.
7. Barriers that may prevent routine logging.

## Research Questions

1. Can participants complete core pain logging tasks without assistance?
2. Do participants understand where their data is stored?
3. Does the local first model increase perceived trust and control?
4. Which parts of the app create confusion, hesitation, or privacy concern?
5. Do participants feel confident using the app for repeated documentation?
6. Do participants feel safe previewing or exporting a report for medical, disability, workplace, legal, or advocacy contexts?

## Study Design

This study uses a mixed methods design:

1. One on one remote usability sessions.
2. Task based app exploration.
3. Moderator observation.
4. Short post task ratings.
5. Semi structured interview.
6. Optional follow up questions.

Each session will last approximately 45 to 60 minutes.

## Participants

### Target Sample

8 to 12 adult participants.

This sample size is intended for an exploratory usability pilot. It is not intended to establish clinical effectiveness or population level outcomes.

### Inclusion Criteria

Participants must:

1. Be 18 years of age or older.
2. Have lived experience with chronic pain, recurring pain, disability related symptoms, or ongoing symptom documentation.
3. Be able to participate in a remote session.
4. Be able to provide informed consent.
5. Be comfortable using a phone, tablet, laptop, or desktop device.

### Exclusion Criteria

Participants will not be included if:

1. They are under 18.
2. They cannot provide informed consent.
3. Participation would create obvious distress or risk.
4. They are unable to complete a remote session with available accommodations.

Participants do not need to disclose a diagnosis, medical history, legal history, income status, or government benefit status.

## Recruitment

Participants will be recruited through:

1. Community outreach.
2. Existing networks.
3. Online posts.
4. Disability, chronic pain, privacy, or self documentation communities where appropriate.

Recruitment materials will clearly state:

1. Participation is voluntary.
2. The app is being tested for usability and trust, not treatment.
3. Participants may use fictional or sample pain data.
4. No full medical history is required.
5. Participants may stop at any time.

## Procedure

### 1. Introduction

The moderator will explain:

1. The purpose of the study.
2. What the participant will be asked to do.
3. That the study is not medical care.
4. That the participant may skip questions or stop at any time.
5. That fictional or approximate information may be used.

### 2. Consent

The participant will review and complete the consent form before beginning.

Separate consent will be requested for:

1. Audio recording.
2. Use of deidentified quotes.
3. Future contact.

Participants may participate without agreeing to audio recording, quote use, or future contact.

### 3. Pre Session Questionnaire

Participants may be asked brief questions about:

1. Prior pain tracking experience.
2. Current tracking method, if any.
3. Baseline privacy concern.
4. Device type used for the session.
5. Accessibility needs relevant to the session.

No detailed medical history will be collected.

### 4. App Tasks

Participants will complete a guided task sequence:

1. Explore the dashboard.
2. Log a new pain entry.
3. Review a past entry.
4. Find privacy and data storage information.
5. Preview or generate a report.
6. Recover from a mistake.
7. Provide open feedback.

Participants may use sample, fictional, approximate, or non sensitive information.

### 5. Observation and Measurement

During each task, the moderator will record:

1. Task completion.
2. Time on task.
3. Errors or retries.
4. Moderator assists.
5. Confusion points.
6. Privacy or trust comments.
7. Signs of cognitive burden or discomfort.
8. Participant comments during the task.

### 6. Post Task Ratings

Participants will complete short rating questions on:

1. Ease of use.
2. Trust.
3. Privacy confidence.
4. Privacy anxiety.
5. Cognitive load.
6. Export confidence.
7. Perceived safety.
8. Likelihood of routine use.

### 7. Semi Structured Interview

Participants will be asked open ended questions such as:

1. What was easiest?
2. What was most confusing?
3. Did the local first privacy model affect your trust?
4. What privacy concerns remained?
5. Would you use this regularly?
6. What would make you stop using it?
7. Would you trust the report for an appointment or documentation process?
8. What should be changed before testing with more people?

### 8. Debrief

At the end of the session, the moderator will:

1. Thank the participant.
2. Remind them they can request withdrawal according to the consent terms.
3. Confirm whether they consent to future contact.
4. Provide contact information for study questions.

## Data Collected

The study may collect:

1. Participant ID.
2. Session date.
3. Recruitment channel.
4. Device type.
5. Task completion records.
6. Task timing.
7. Error and assistance counts.
8. Survey ratings.
9. Moderator notes.
10. Interview notes.
11. Optional audio recordings.
12. Optional deidentified quotes.

The study will not intentionally collect:

1. Full medical histories.
2. Diagnostic records.
3. Legal records.
4. Government benefit records.
5. Employment records.
6. Financial records.
7. Raw private pain diaries beyond what the participant chooses to enter during testing.

## Data Handling

PainTracker app data remains on the participant's device unless the participant chooses to export or share it.

Research data is separate from app data.

Research data will be handled as follows:

1. Consent forms will be stored separately from study notes.
2. Participant names will not be used in analysis files.
3. Participant IDs will be used instead of names.
4. Notes will be deidentified where possible.
5. Audio recordings, if collected, will be stored securely.
6. Deidentified survey and task data may be used in aggregate reporting.
7. Direct quotes will only be used if quote consent is granted.
8. Identifiable data will be deleted according to the retention period stated in the consent form.

## Privacy and Confidentiality

Participant identity will not be included in public reports.

Reports may include:

1. Aggregate task completion results.
2. Average ratings.
3. Common usability issues.
4. Deidentified themes.
5. Deidentified quotes, only with permission.
6. Interface recommendations.

Reports will not include information that directly identifies participants.

## Risks

Risks are expected to be low, but may include:

1. Mild discomfort when thinking about pain, privacy, health documentation, disability, or prior institutional experiences.
2. Frustration if the app is confusing.
3. Concern about data storage or accidental disclosure.
4. Fatigue from completing tasks.

Participants may pause, skip a task, skip a question, or stop the session at any time.

## Risk Mitigation

The study reduces risk by:

1. Allowing fictional or sample data.
2. Avoiding collection of detailed medical histories.
3. Making participation voluntary.
4. Allowing withdrawal.
5. Separating consent data from study notes.
6. Using deidentified participant IDs.
7. Avoiding unnecessary sensitive questions.
8. Giving participants control over recording, quote use, and future contact.

## Benefits

There may be no direct personal benefit.

Potential broader benefits include improving PainTracker and contributing to safer local first tools for people who document pain, symptoms, disability, or sensitive personal information.

## Compensation

No compensation will be provided.

Because no compensation is offered, participants should not be pressured to continue. They may stop early without penalty.

## Analysis Plan

Quantitative analysis will summarize:

1. Task completion rates.
2. Time on task.
3. Error counts.
4. Moderator assists.
5. Rating distributions.
6. Composite scores for usability, trust, privacy anxiety, cognitive load, routine use, and perceived safety.

Qualitative analysis will identify recurring themes related to:

1. Navigation confusion.
2. Privacy uncertainty.
3. Local storage trust.
4. Export confidence.
5. Cognitive overload.
6. Accessibility barriers.
7. Routine logging support.
8. Failure state confusion.
9. Data exposure fear.
10. Suggested improvements.

Findings will be mapped to Protective Computing principles:

1. Local authority.
2. Exposure minimization.
3. Consent clarity.
4. Reversible state.
5. Explicit degradation.
6. Cognitive protection.
7. Trust under stress.

## Success Criteria

The pilot will be considered successful if:

1. At least 80 percent of participants complete the core logging task.
2. Average core logging time is under 5 minutes.
3. At least 80 percent of participants correctly understand local data storage.
4. Average trust score is 4 out of 5 or higher.
5. Average perceived safety score is 4 out of 5 or higher.
6. Average privacy anxiety score is 2 out of 5 or lower.
7. No critical safety, privacy, or data loss concern is observed.

The pilot will require redesign before expansion if:

1. Participants cannot explain where their data is stored.
2. Multiple participants believe data is uploaded when it is not.
3. Navigation blocks core logging.
4. Exporting creates significant confusion.
5. Participants feel pressured to disclose more than they want.
6. Any participant reports feeling unsafe, exposed, or misled.

## Limitations

This study is exploratory.

It does not prove:

1. Clinical effectiveness.
2. Pain reduction.
3. Improved medical outcomes.
4. Long term adherence.
5. Superiority over all cloud based apps.

It may provide early evidence about:

1. First use usability.
2. Trust in local first design.
3. Privacy understanding.
4. Perceived safety.
5. Barriers to adoption.
6. Design changes needed before larger testing.

## Ethics Statement

All participants must provide informed consent before taking part.

The study will follow a minimum necessary data approach. Participants will not be required to disclose private medical details. Participants may use fictional or sample entries. Participation is voluntary, and participants may stop at any time.

The research process should reflect the same protective principles being tested by the app: local authority, exposure minimization, consent clarity, reversibility, explicit degradation, and cognitive protection.
